## Overview

Airbnb connects guests looking for short-term stays with hosts offering their spaces. Behind a deceptively simple search box sits a two-sided marketplace that must solve four hard problems at once: geospatial and filtered search over millions of listings, concurrent availability to prevent double-booking, split payments with escrow-style holds, and a trust system built on two-way reviews.

Each side of the marketplace — supply (hosts and listings) and demand (guests) — has different scaling pressures, and the system's hardest edges are where they meet: the booking transaction. A search can be eventually consistent and cached aggressively, but a booking must be strictly consistent, because selling the same night twice is the one mistake a marketplace cannot tolerate.

## Key Components

<ComponentList>
<ComponentCard name="Search Service">
Handles geospatial and filtered queries over listings. Reads from a denormalized search index (Elasticsearch-style) optimized for location bounding boxes, date ranges, and facet filters. Listing detail pages are cached aggressively because reads vastly outnumber writes.
</ComponentCard>
<ComponentCard name="Availability Service">
The source of truth for which nights are bookable. Exposes atomic "reserve these nights" operations backed by row-level locking or optimistic concurrency on calendar-day rows. This is the component that prevents double-booking.
</ComponentCard>
<ComponentCard name="Booking Service">
Owns the reservation state machine (pending, confirmed, paid, canceled, completed). Orchestrates the transaction across availability, payment, and pricing. Emits booking events to Kafka for downstream consumers.
</ComponentCard>
<ComponentCard name="Payment Service">
Charges the guest, holds funds in escrow, and schedules the host payout after check-in. Every charge is keyed by an idempotency token so retries from flaky clients never double-charge. Handles split payouts, platform fees, and taxes.
</ComponentCard>
<ComponentCard name="Review Service">
Collects two-way reviews (guest reviews host, host reviews guest) with a delayed reveal: neither review is visible until both are submitted or a window expires. This prevents retaliation bias.
</ComponentCard>
<ComponentCard name="Pricing Service">
Computes nightly prices and suggests dynamic ("Smart") pricing based on demand, seasonality, local events, and listing quality. Runs mostly as a read for search results and a write for host-facing suggestions.
</ComponentCard>
</ComponentList>

## Search & Discovery

Search is the marketplace's front door, and it is read-heavy: millions of guests browse for every one that books.

**The 0->1 default:**
A single PostgreSQL table with `lat`/`lng` columns, filtered by a bounding box and guest count, is enough to launch. It is simple, transactional, and good enough for a few thousand listings.

**The breakpoint:**
Once the listing catalog grows past tens of thousands and filters get richer (amenities, price ranges, instant-book), the database scan-and-filter pattern becomes too slow. The system evolves to a dedicated inverted search index that denormalizes each listing into a searchable document with geospatial fields and facets.

**Cost drivers:**
- Index freshness lag: a new listing may take seconds to minutes to appear, which is acceptable for search but not for booking.
- Egress and compute from re-ranking millions of candidates per query.
- Cache invalidation when a host edits a listing or a night gets booked.

## Booking & Availability Concurrency

This is the system's strict-consistency core. Two guests requesting the same listing for overlapping dates must never both succeed.

**The 0->1 default:**
A `calendar_day` row per listing per night with a `status` column. Booking a stay runs inside a transaction that locks the relevant rows and flips them from `available` to `reserved`. Row-level locking is correct and simple.

**The breakpoint:**
Lock contention appears when popular listings or high-traffic dates (holidays, events) attract many simultaneous requests. Contention turns into latency and deadlocks. The system evolves to **optimistic concurrency**: read the calendar versions, attempt a conditional update, and retry on conflict. For high-demand listings, a short-lived **reservation hold** in Redis reserves the nights for a few minutes while payment completes, so the database lock is held only briefly.

<Mermaid chart={`graph TB
    A[Guest requests dates] --> B[Read calendar days + version]
    B --> C{All nights available?}
    C -->|no| FAIL[Reject - unavailable]
    C -->|yes| D[Place Redis hold - TTL minutes]
    D --> E[Charge payment]
    E --> F{Payment OK?}
    F -->|no| REL[Release hold]
    F -->|yes| G[Atomic conditional update
    WHERE status = available
    AND version = expected]
    G --> H{Rows updated?}
    H -->|no - conflict| RETRY[Retry / reject]
    H -->|yes| OK[Confirm booking]`} caption="Booking flow with optimistic concurrency and a reservation hold" />

**Why this matters:**
The hold decouples the slow, failure-prone payment step from the fast availability check. The database only commits the final state change, and the conditional `WHERE` clause makes double-booking impossible without holding a long lock.

## Payments & Split Payouts

Payments in a marketplace are harder than in a normal store because money flows to a third party (the host), and the platform takes a cut.

**The flow:**
1. Guest is charged the full amount at booking confirmation.
2. Funds are held in escrow — the host is not paid yet.
3. After check-in (or a defined window), the host payout is scheduled.
4. The platform fee and taxes are split off; the remainder is sent to the host's payout method.

**Idempotency is non-negotiable.** Every charge carries an idempotency key (often derived from the booking ID and attempt number). A retry from a dropped connection or a webhook redelivery must return the original result, not create a second charge. This is the same pattern that makes retries safe across the reliability lessons.

**Cost drivers & breakpoints:**
- Payment processor fees per transaction — significant at marketplace scale.
- Payout failures and retries requiring reconciliation jobs.
- Multi-currency conversion and region-specific tax rules, which push the payment service toward a rules engine rather than hardcoded logic.

## Reviews & Trust

Trust is the marketplace's moat, and reviews are its currency. Airbnb uses **two-way reviews with delayed reveal**:

- After a stay, both the guest and the host can leave a review.
- Neither review is published until both have submitted, or a 14-day window closes.
- This prevents the second reviewer from retaliating based on what the first wrote.

**Consistency model:**
Reviews are eventually consistent. They are written to the primary database, published by a scheduled job when the reveal condition is met, and propagated to listing pages and search ranking signals. A review being briefly delayed is acceptable; a booking being double-sold is not. This split — strict consistency for transactions, eventual consistency for everything else — is the central architectural decision of the whole system.
