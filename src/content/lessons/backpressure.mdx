## What it is

Backpressure is a feedback mechanism that prevents producers from overwhelming consumers. Instead of accepting unlimited work, the system slows, buffers, sheds, or rejects work.

## Why it matters

Overload often cascades. Queues grow, memory fills, latency spikes, retries multiply, and healthy services become unhealthy. Backpressure keeps overload bounded.

## How it works

Signals include queue depth, consumer lag, CPU, memory, connection buffers, and downstream errors. Producers respond by reducing rate, batching, pausing reads, or returning a retryable error.

## When to use it

Use it in queues, streaming APIs, WebSocket gateways, media pipelines, and inference schedulers.

## Tradeoffs / failure modes

Too much buffering hides problems and increases latency. Too little buffering rejects work prematurely. The product must decide what to degrade first.

## Tiny example

If a WebSocket client's send buffer grows too large, the gateway stops reading new events for that connection or disconnects it.
